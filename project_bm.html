BodyMirror - Smart TV interaction

UX Research, 2015.3

To build a design principle, I initiatively selected the HCI class for computer science majors,
during which I equipped myself with graphical user interface principles and Fitts’ Law,
among others.

In the process of completing the course project of designing a new interaction means with smart TV,
I made significant contributions to the formation of an innovative idea.

Inspired by Dr. Harrison’s OmniTouch project and BayMax,
I proposed making users utilize an image of their body as an input touch screen
to interact with displays of smart TV, named as “BodyMirror.”

introduction

BodyMirror regards a user's torso as a multi-touch input surface mirrored from the remote display.
Users use their fingers to interact on the torso surface just as they do on a real physical touch screen.

The “mirror” depicts the spatial correspondence between the display and our torso:
for example,
a target on the upper left of the display corresponds to the location near our left shoulder.
After spotting a target of interest on the display,
a user mentally translates the target to the corresponding position on her torso.
Then, she moves her finger onto the torso surface via kinesthetic perception,
and adjusts by tactile feedback to estimate the target position.
The last contact point specifies the final hit location.

design

BodyMirror provides an intuitive, lightweight solution to eyes-free indirect touch.
Human’s torso is an appropriate area for touch interactions ,
with a large available input surface and small movement compared to other parts of body.
Users are allowed to do a single- or double- tap to target, drag to move, pinch to scale
and perform bimanual gestures.
In addition, users can always maintain their visual attention on the display, achieving a smooth interaction experience.

development

To recognize the movement, we built a depth–skin mixture model
from the data sensed by Kinect to distract hands and fingers from background graph.
